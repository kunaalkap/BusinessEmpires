Background

The East India Company (EIC), chartered in 1600, was one of the most consequential commercial and political organisations in world history. At the intersection of trade, empire, and law, its internal governance mechanisms — including committees responsible for managing disputes and lawsuits — offer a uniquely detailed window into how early modern corporations exercised power.

This project focuses on the Committee of Lawsuit, a standing body within the EIC responsible for overseeing legal disputes, contract enforcement, cargo claims, and matters of corporate liability. Its minute books and session notes form the primary corpus for this research.

The Corpus: 164 Committee Notes

The core dataset consists of 164 individual meeting records produced by the Committee of Lawsuit. Each note documents:

  • Parties involved in legal proceedings (merchants, captains, company officers, third-party claimants)
  • Nature of disputes (ship seizures, debt recovery, cargo damage, contractual violations)
  • Procedural actions taken (referrals, delays, settlements, external court involvement)
  • Names, roles, and recurring actors across cases
  • Temporal markers enabling timeline reconstruction
ℹ

These records are being systematically transcribed, cleaned, and coded to enable both qualitative close reading and quantitative computational analysis.

Methodology

This project employs a mixed-methods approach combining traditional historical analysis with digital humanities and computational techniques:

Close Reading

Each of the 164 records is annotated and coded for key entities, legal categories, and outcomes.

Timeline Reconstruction

Temporal data reconstructs the chronological arc of disputes and institutional activity.

Word Frequency & NLP

Computational text analysis surfaces dominant terminology and thematic clusters.

Network Analysis

Graph analysis maps relationships between named actors, revealing clusters of influence.

Applications & Use Cases

Analytical findings serve multiple scholarly and practical purposes:

  • Legal history: Tracing the development of commercial dispute resolution in early modern England
  • Economic history: Understanding how the EIC managed financial risk through legal mechanisms
  • Corporate governance: Examining precedents for internal institutional oversight
  • Social history: Mapping the human networks that operated within and around the Company
  • Digital humanities: Demonstrating the application of ML and NLP to early modern archival material

Inquiry Framework

Research Questions

The four core investigative threads guiding this project.

01 Legal History

How did the EIC's Committee of Lawsuit function as a legal institution within the broader English legal system of the early modern period?

The records show the Committee operating as a quasi-autonomous legal body, handling disputes internally before any referral to state courts. The reveals a dramatic peak in 1704 when internal Company Committee actions dominate the data, pointing to the EIC managing an exceptional volume of disputes without external referral. The traces how the boundary between internal and external jurisdiction moved across the full two-decade study window.

02 Social Networks

What professional and social networks are embedded within the lawsuit records, and how did these networks shape the outcomes of disputes?

Named-entity extraction across the 164 records surfaces a tight cluster of recurring legal practitioners. The shows Mr Hungerford, Mr Rowe, Mr Coulson, Mr Dubois, and Mr Dodd all peaking together around 1704, a pattern that points to coordinated legal strategy more than coincidental caseload overlap. Whether these practitioners formed a cohesive faction within the Committee's proceedings is a question the broader network analysis continues to pursue.

03 Governance

Where did corporate governance end and the external legal system begin — and how stable was that boundary over time?

The makes this boundary instability legible. The relative share of Company Committee versus State Court actions fluctuates substantially across the period, and a persistent band of jurisdictionally ambiguous records runs throughout the dataset. Following the 1704 peak, the registers a measurable drift toward State Court involvement, consistent with external legal institutions progressively encroaching on the EIC's claimed jurisdiction.

04 Legal Themes

What legal themes — particularly ship seizures and dispute processes — recur most frequently, and what do their patterns reveal about the Company's priorities?

The confirms that Committee Governance (220 mentions) and Maritime Seizures & Ship Litigation (134 mentions) dominate the corpus. Ship-related disputes were among the most pressing legal concerns of the period. The further tracks how these themes distribute across time, clarifying whether ship litigation and governance disputes were concentrated in particular years or dispersed more evenly across the Company's records.

Connecting Thread

These four questions share a common problem. The East India Company was a commercial body that accumulated judicial authority it was never formally granted, and the minutes of the Committee of Lawsuit document how that authority was exercised in practice. How the Company managed its legal proceedings, whom it recruited to conduct them, and why its apparatus came to resemble a state institution more than a trading house are questions that historians of early modern England have only partially addressed. The records studied here offer a rare ground-level view of that process.
